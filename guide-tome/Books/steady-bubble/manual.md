# PART I — ADMINISTRATION

## Chapter 1 — Purpose and Scope

### 1.1 Authority

This document constitutes the Standard Operating Procedures (SOP) for NATO submarine operations conducted under STEADY BUBBLE tasking. It is the authoritative reference for all watchstanders, department heads, and the Commanding Officer (CO) for the conduct of normal and emergency evolutions aboard assigned vessels.

### 1.2 Applicability

These procedures apply to all submarine classes assigned to this operational theatre:

| Hull | Class | Propulsion | Nation |
|---|---|---|---|
| SSN-700 USS Dallas | Los Angeles (688i) | Nuclear | United States |
| S107 HMS Trafalgar | Trafalgar | Nuclear | United Kingdom |
| S126 HMS Swiftsure | Swiftsure | Nuclear | United Kingdom |
| SSN-22 USS Connecticut | Seawolf | Nuclear | United States |
| U-36 | Type 209/1400mod | Diesel-Electric | Germany |

Where procedures differ between nuclear and diesel-electric propulsion, or between specific vessel classes, this is noted explicitly.

### 1.3 Rules of Engagement

All procedures assume Cold War-era rules of engagement against Soviet naval forces in open-ocean and littoral environments. Merchant contacts are non-combatant and shall not be engaged.

### 1.4 Manual Organisation

- **Part I** — Administration, definitions, vessel data
- **Part II** — Normal operations: navigation, propulsion, sonar, fire control
- **Part III** — Weapons employment procedures
- **Part IV** — Damage control and emergency procedures
- **Part V** — Threat assessment and tactical doctrine

# PART II — NORMAL OPERATIONS

## Chapter 5 — Helm and Navigation

### 5.1 Evolution: Changing Speed

**What happens:** The Engine Order Telegraph (EOT) transmits the ordered speed to the Maneuvering watchstanders. The throttleman adjusts main steam throttle valves to increase or decrease turbine output. Ship's speed converges on the ordered speed over a 45-second time constant — the boat does not achieve ordered speed instantly.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** announces the new speed order to the Control Room.
- **Helmsman** acknowledges the order and reads back the new speed.
- **Throttleman (Maneuvering)** adjusts steam admission to the main turbines.
- **Sonar Supervisor** notes the speed change for self-noise assessment.

**Player command:** Press **D** to increase ordered speed by 1 kt per press. Press **A** to decrease by 1 kt per press. Both keys repeat on hold for continuous adjustment.

**Standard Engine Orders:**

| Order | Speed | Tactical Use |
|---|---|---|
| ALL STOP | 0 kt | Dead in the water. No plane authority. Drift only. |
| AHEAD CREEP | 3 kt | Minimum steerage. Near-silent. Maximum passive sonar range. |
| AHEAD SLOW | 7 kt | Quiet patrol. Good sonar, low noise. Standard patrol speed. |
| AHEAD STANDARD | 14 kt | Economical transit. Moderate sonar degradation. |
| AHEAD FULL | 20 kt | Maximum sustained speed. Significant self-masking. |
| AHEAD FLANK | 28 kt | Emergency speed. Effectively deaf. Severe noise penalty. |

**Cautions:**
- Speed is the primary determinant of acoustic signature and sonar effectiveness. Every speed increase trades detection range for transit speed.
- At 3-5 kt, the boat is near-silent with maximum passive sonar range.
- At 10+ kt, passive sonar is seriously degraded. At 20+ kt, the boat is effectively deaf.
- Flank speed adds a noise boost of +0.42 (688i) above the normal flow noise curve. This is audible to every enemy in the operational area.
- Damage to propulsion, main turbines, or steering may impose speed restrictions. A reactor SCRAM limits the boat to 3 kt on the Emergency Propulsion Motor.
- The Type 209 maximum speed is 8 kt on battery, 12 kt on flank. Endurance is approximately 40 minutes at 3 kt, 15 minutes at 8 kt.

### 5.2 Evolution: Changing Depth

**What happens:** The Diving Officer orders a new depth. The planesman adjusts the fairwater and stern planes to produce the commanded pitch angle. Main ballast tank fill is adjusted via HPA to fine-tune buoyancy. The submarine descends or ascends to the ordered depth and levels off.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** announces the ordered depth.
- **Diving Officer** repeats the order and directs the planesman.
- **Planesman** adjusts bow and stern planes to establish the desired depth rate.
- **Chief of the Watch** monitors ballast tank fill and adjusts trim as required.
- **Sonar Supervisor** notes the depth change for thermal layer awareness.

**Player command:** Press **S** to increase ordered depth by 10m per press. Press **W** to decrease ordered depth by 10m per press. Both keys repeat on hold.

**Depth control characteristics:**
- Hydroplanes require forward speed to be effective. Below 10 kt, plane authority degrades linearly. At zero speed, planes are ineffective and buoyancy alone controls depth.
- Maximum depth rate: 1.8 m/s at full plane authority.
- Depth convergence time constant: 8 seconds.
- The depth controller uses a two-zone approach: full authority outside 15m of the target depth, proportional settle within 15m.
- Flooding, trim imbalance, and HPA depletion all degrade depth control authority.

### 5.3 Depth Envelope

Every vessel class has a defined depth envelope. The CO is personally accountable for depth excursions beyond the Diving Limit. Exceeding the envelope produces increasingly serious consequences.

| Zone | Definition | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Safe Diving Depth (SDD)** | Maximum depth for routine operations | Normal operations. No restrictions. |
| **Diving Limit (DL)** | Certified maximum depth | Warning to Conn. CO accountability. |
| **Design Depth (DD)** | Structural design limit — emergency use only | Formal warning. CO decision required. |
| **Collapse Depth** | Hull begins structural deformation | Progressive seep flooding initiates. Hull creaking audible. |
| **Crush Depth** | Catastrophic structural failure | Immediate loss of the boat with all hands. |

**Critical note:** Crush depth is not fixed. Structural damage from weapon impacts reduces the effective crush depth. A boat with two serious hits may have its crush depth reduced to approximately 72% of the nominal value. The crew receives no precise indication of the new limit — hull creaking is the only warning.

**Depth envelope by class:**

| Vessel | SDD | DL | DD | Crush |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| USS Dallas (688i) | 300m | 400m | 450m | 520m |
| HMS Trafalgar | 300m | 400m | 450m | 520m |
| HMS Swiftsure | 260m | 350m | 400m | 465m |
| USS Connecticut (Seawolf) | 365m | 480m | 530m | 605m |
| U-36 (Type 209) | 190m | 250m | 280m | 325m |

### 5.4 Evolution: Waypoint Navigation

**What happens:** The Navigator plots the new course on the tactical display. The OOW orders the new heading. The Helmsman puts the rudder over to achieve the ordered course. The submarine turns toward the waypoint at a speed-dependent rate.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Navigator** plots the waypoint and calculates the intercept heading.
- **OOW** orders the new course.
- **Helmsman** applies rudder and steers to the ordered heading.
- **Sonar Supervisor** notes the course change — turning generates a noise transient of +0.07.

**Player command:** Left-click on the tactical display (ocean area) to place a waypoint. The submarine turns toward the waypoint. Multiple waypoints may be queued — the boat follows them in sequence. Right-click to remove the last waypoint.

**Turn rate characteristics:**
- Maximum turn rate: 2.2 degrees per second at full speed.
- Minimum turn rate: 0.5 degrees per second at creep speed.
- Each turn generates a noise transient of +0.07 per heading change.
- Waypoint reached reports are issued as the submarine passes each waypoint.

### 5.5 Evolution: Emergency Turn

**What happens:** The OOW orders hard rudder. The Helmsman puts the rudder to 35 degrees — full deflection. The submarine executes a rapid course change. The evolution is violent — all personnel brace. All torpedo guidance wires are severed by the hull stress. The towed array, if deployed, is damaged or destroyed by the cable dynamics.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders "HARD RUDDER" and announces the emergency turn to all stations.
- **Helmsman** applies full rudder deflection (35 degrees).
- **Weapons Officer (WEPS)** reports loss of all torpedo wires.
- **Sonar Supervisor** reports towed array status (damaged/destroyed if deployed).
- **All hands** brace for the manoeuvre.

**Player command:** Press **Q**.

**Parameters:**
- Duration: 2.2 seconds of hard-over rudder.
- Cooldown: 8 seconds before the next emergency turn is available.
- Noise transient: +0.28 — audible to all enemies at close-to-moderate range.
- Wire consequence: All torpedo guidance wires are severed immediately.
- Towed array consequence: Damages or destroys the array if deployed.
- Combined manoeuvre risk: Executing both emergency turn and crash dive within rapid succession at speeds above 20 kt creates a 45% probability of reactor SCRAM due to mechanical shock to the reactor control rod assembly.

**When to execute:** To break the track of an incoming torpedo when countermeasures have failed. To rapidly change heading when a weapons solution on the enemy requires a new geometry. Not to be used casually — the noise transient, wire severance, and towed array risk make this a last-resort manoeuvre.

### 5.6 Evolution: Crash Dive

**What happens:** The OOW orders emergency deep. The Diving Officer slams the planes to maximum dive angle. The EOT rings up flank speed. The submarine plunges at 3.5 times the normal depth rate. Depth is ordered to current depth plus 600m (the system does not literally descend 600m — it orders maximum depth change). The evolution is intended to rapidly cross below the thermal layer or to open vertical separation from a surface threat.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders "EMERGENCY DEEP" and announces the crash dive to all stations.
- **Diving Officer** slams planes to maximum dive angle.
- **Throttleman** rings up flank speed.
- **WEPS** reports loss of all torpedo wires.
- **Sonar Supervisor** reports towed array status.
- **Chief of the Watch** monitors depth gauge — crush depth awareness is critical.
- **All hands** brace.

**Player command:** Press **C**.

**Parameters:**
- Duration: 3.5 seconds of maximum dive rate (3.5x normal).
- Cooldown: 12 seconds before the next crash dive is available.
- Depth convergence override: time constant reduced to 0.4 seconds (nearly instant response).
- Noise transient: +0.35 — larger than an emergency turn.
- Wire consequence: All torpedo guidance wires severed immediately.
- Towed array consequence: Damages or destroys the array if deployed.
- Combined manoeuvre risk: Same 45% SCRAM probability as above when combined with emergency turn at speed.

**When to execute:** To cross below the thermal layer when enemy torpedo seekers are active above it. To rapidly gain depth when the surface picture is hostile. Not to be used casually. The CO must be aware of the depth envelope — a crash dive initiated at 350m in a boat with a 400m diving limit will push the submarine into the danger zone.

## Chapter 6 — Propulsion Systems

### 6.1 Nuclear Propulsion — Normal Operations

The 688i, Trafalgar, Swiftsure, and Seawolf classes are nuclear-powered. The pressurised water reactor generates steam to drive the main turbines, which turn the propeller shaft through a reduction gearbox. The reactor provides unlimited endurance at any depth with no requirement to surface for propulsion.

The ship's battery charges automatically when the reactor is online. The battery drains only during a reactor SCRAM.

### 6.2 Evolution: Reactor SCRAM

**What happens:** A SCRAM is the emergency rapid insertion of all control rods into the reactor core, halting the fission chain reaction. The reactor does not shut down or go cold — it continues to produce significant thermal energy from fission product decay heat and remains fully dependent on the primary coolant system for continued cooling. A true cold shutdown takes days to weeks; a SCRAM is an emergency protective action measured in seconds.

What a SCRAM denies is the ability to sustain steam generation required to drive the main turbines. Propulsion is lost because there is no longer sufficient thermal output to produce steam at the rate the turbines demand — not because the reactor has ceased to function.

The reactor plant remains an active system throughout a SCRAM. Primary coolant must continue circulating. The pressuriser must maintain system pressure. Failure of these systems during a SCRAM is a compounding emergency.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Reactor Operator (Maneuvering)** reports "SCRAM, SCRAM, SCRAM" on all circuits.
- **Throttleman** confirms loss of steam to main turbines.
- **Electrical Operator** shifts propulsion to the Emergency Propulsion Motor (EPM).
- **Engineering Officer of the Watch (EOOW)** begins SCRAM recovery procedures. 75-second countdown to restart.
- **OOW** reduces ordered speed to 3 kt (EPM maximum). Assesses depth control — plane authority at 3 kt is marginal.
- **Diving Officer** monitors depth. If flooding is concurrent, buoyancy may be the only depth control remaining.

**Causes:**
- Primary coolant system failure not corrected within the 45-second countdown.
- Mechanical shock from combined emergency manoeuvres at high speed (45% probability).
- Direct weapon impact on reactor or primary coolant systems (8% probability per severe hit).

**Effects:**
- Propulsion limited to 3 kt — EPM only (battery-powered).
- Battery begins draining at 0.0020 per second.
- 75-second restart window before the reactor can be brought back to power.
- All active propulsion casualties (coolant leak, steam leak, turbine trip) are cleared by the rod insertion.

**Player response:** Reduce speed to 3 kt or less (the system will enforce this). Monitor depth — at 3 kt, plane authority is minimal. If flooding is concurrent, prepare for possible emergency blow. Wait 75 seconds for reactor restart.

### 6.3 Evolution: Primary Coolant Leak

**What happens:** The primary coolant piping develops a leak under sustained stress from high-speed operations at depth. After 15 seconds of continuous flank-speed running, the risk of a coolant leak begins at 0.8% per second. The probability triples if the primary coolant system is already in a degraded state.

Once detected, the Reactor Operator commences a 45-second countdown. If the engineering crew cannot repair the leak before the countdown expires, reactor safety interlocks trigger an automatic SCRAM.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Reactor Operator** detects the leak and begins the 45-second countdown. Reports to Conn.
- **EOOW** dispatches repair crew. Recommends speed reduction to the OOW.
- **OOW** orders speed reduction — this is the single most important action. Low speed (below one-third maximum) provides 65% repair probability and runs the countdown 50% slower. High speed provides only 30% repair probability and runs the countdown 50% faster.

**Player response:** Immediately reduce speed. Press **A** repeatedly to reduce to 7 kt or below. The countdown timer and repair probability are directly linked to speed. If the repair succeeds, operations resume normally. If the countdown expires, the reactor SCRAMS.

### 6.4 Evolution: Steam Leak

**What happens:** A steam leak occurs when weapon impact damages the main turbines or pressuriser in an already-degraded state. 12% probability per hit under these conditions. A jet of superheated steam enters the engine room.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **EOOW** reports the steam leak. Speed automatically restricted to 7 kt.
- **Engine room watchstanders** isolate the leak. Auto-repair in 30-60 seconds.
- **OOW** acknowledges the speed restriction. No crew intervention required beyond monitoring.

**Player response:** Acknowledge the 7 kt speed cap. Wait 30-60 seconds for automatic repair. No action required.

### 6.5 Evolution: Turbine Trip

**What happens:** A turbine trip occurs when a nearby explosion (15% probability per hit) or sudden throttle change exceeding 10 kt per second (20% probability) causes the main turbines to disconnect from the shaft. Speed is restricted to 12 kt.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Throttleman** reports turbine trip. Speed restricted to 12 kt.
- **EOOW** monitors auto-recovery. 20-30 second recovery time.
- **OOW** acknowledges. Avoids further sudden speed changes during recovery.

**Player response:** Acknowledge the 12 kt speed cap. Avoid rapid speed changes until recovery completes (20-30 seconds). No action required.

### 6.6 Evolution: Reactor Runaway

**What happens:** An uncontrolled power excursion. 8% probability on a severe hit to the reactor or primary coolant system. The safety systems contain the excursion — no direct damage to the boat. The primary danger is the acoustic signature.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Reactor Operator** reports the runaway. Safety interlocks contain the event.
- **Sonar Supervisor** reports that the acoustic transient is audible to enemies at approximately 3,000 wu (30 km / 16 nm).
- **OOW** assesses the tactical situation. Every nearby enemy now has a bearing on the boat.

**Player response:** No mechanical action required. Be aware that all enemies within 3,000 wu have received a large suspicion increase and a precise bearing. Consider evasion procedures.

### 6.7 Diesel-Electric Propulsion — Type 209

The Type 209 operates on battery power when submerged. The battery drains proportionally to speed at 0.00014 per knot per second.

| Speed | Approximate Endurance |
|---|---|
| 3 kt | ~40 minutes |
| 5 kt | ~24 minutes |
| 8 kt (maximum) | ~15 minutes |

Battery warnings are issued at 30%, 20%, and 10% remaining charge.

### 6.8 Evolution: Snorkel Operations (Type 209)

**What happens:** The diesels require an air supply. The snorkel mast is raised to 12m depth. The diesel generators engage and begin recharging the battery. The noise signature is extreme — one of the loudest signatures in the operational theatre.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders "COMMENCE SNORKELLING." Depth ordered to 12m.
- **Chief of the Watch** confirms snorkel mast raised and intake valve open.
- **EOOW** starts diesel generators. Reports charge rate.
- **Sonar Supervisor** reports degradation from snorkel noise — self-masking is severe.
- **OOW** maintains continuous surface watch. Speed capped at 5 kt.

**Player command:** Order depth to 12m (press **W** until depth reads 12m). Snorkelling begins automatically when conditions are met.

**Charge rates:**
- Snorkel charge rate: approximately 0.3% per second.
- Surface charge rate: approximately 0.5% per second.

**Cautions:**
- Snorkel noise is 0.35 — nearly guaranteed detection by any enemy within passive sonar range.
- Speed is capped at 5 kt while snorkelling.
- Snorkel only when the area is confirmed clear of hostile contacts.
- Never snorkel when enemies have bearing (suspicion > 0.22), torpedoes are in the water, or contacts have not been cleared by a passive listen at 3 kt.

### 6.9 Evolution: Dead Battery (Type 209)

**What happens:** Battery reaches zero charge while submerged and not snorkelling. All propulsion ceases. Plane authority is lost. The boat begins sinking.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **EOOW** reports battery exhaustion. All propulsion lost.
- **Diving Officer** reports loss of plane authority. Depth is uncontrolled.
- **OOW** immediately orders depth to 12m for emergency snorkel. If current depth exceeds snorkel mast limits, the situation may be unrecoverable.

**Player response:** Immediately order depth to 12m (press **W** repeatedly). If the boat is too deep for the snorkel mast, prepare for emergency blow.

### 6.10 Evolution: Silent Running

**What happens:** The OOW orders "RIG FOR SILENT RUNNING." Non-essential machinery is shut down. Crew movement is restricted. The acoustic signature is reduced by approximately 45%. Speed is capped at 8 kt.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders silent running. Reports to all stations.
- **EOOW** secures non-essential machinery and ventilation.
- **Chief of the Watch** confirms all non-essential systems secured.
- **Sonar Supervisor** notes the reduction in self-noise. Reports improved passive detection capability.

**Player command:** Press **Z** to toggle silent running on or off.

**Parameters:**
- Noise multiplier: 0.55 (45% reduction).
- Speed cap: 8 kt.
- Cannot be activated if the Control Room is non-functional.

**Tactical note:** Silent running does not eliminate noise — it reduces it. At 8 kt in silent running, the boat is still audible at moderate range. For minimum noise, combine silent running with creep speed (3 kt). At this combination, the noise floor is approximately 0.022-0.035 depending on vessel.

## Chapter 7 — Sonar Operations

### 7.1 Hull-Mounted Array — Passive Detection

**What it is:** A large-aperture hydrophone array mounted in the bow of the submarine. It listens for sounds produced by other vessels — propulsion plant noise, auxiliary machinery, and hydrodynamic flow.

**How detection works:** Every vessel in the water produces noise. The hull array detects this noise and presents it to the sonar operator as bearing lines on the tactical display. Detection depends on the balance between the target's noise and the submarine's own noise (self-masking), modified by range, geometry, thermal layer effects, and watch fatigue.

**Effective range by condition:**

| Condition | Approximate Range |
|---|---|
| Loud target, own ship quiet | ~28 km / 15 nm (2,800 wu) |
| Moderate target, patrol speed | ~15-20 km / 8-11 nm |
| Quiet target, own ship loud | < 5 km / 3 nm |
| Across thermal layer | Range reduced by approximately 40% |

### 7.2 Speed and Self-Masking

The submarine's own flow noise masks incoming signals. This is the most important trade-off in submarine warfare.

| Own Speed | Effect on Sonar |
|---|---|
| 0-3 kt | Near-maximum passive range. Self-masking negligible. |
| 4-7 kt | Slight degradation beginning — still operationally effective. |
| 8-10 kt | Noticeable reduction in detection range. |
| 10-15 kt | Seriously degraded — close-range detections only. |
| 15+ kt | Effectively deaf — 80-90% reduction in capability. |

Self-masking onset and full-deafness speeds vary by vessel:

| Vessel | Deafness Onset | Full Deafness |
|---|---|---|
| USS Dallas (688i) | 4 kt | 10 kt |
| HMS Trafalgar | 5 kt | 12 kt |
| HMS Swiftsure | 4 kt | 11 kt |
| USS Connecticut (Seawolf) | 5 kt | 13 kt |
| U-36 (Type 209) | 6 kt | 14 kt |

### 7.3 Baffle Zone

The hull array has a blind sector astern, masked by the submarine's own propeller noise.

- At rest: approximately +/-15 degrees from dead astern (varies by class).
- At speed: expands by approximately 1.5 degrees per knot (maximum +/-45 degrees).
- Contacts in the baffle zone cannot be detected by the hull array.

The towed array partially compensates — see Section 7.5. However, it has its own dead cone of +/-28 degrees off the stern.

Periodic baffle-clearing manoeuvres (turning 30+ degrees off heading to listen astern) are recommended during patrol.

### 7.4 Watch Fatigue

Sonar operators on watch fatigue over time. At maximum fatigue, detection probability is reduced by up to 40% and bearing estimates become noisier.

**Player awareness:** The watch rotation indicator on the HUD shows the active watch (A or B) and current fatigue level. Rotating the watch resets fatigue but requires a brief handover period. Both watches are called during action stations, which doubles available crew but accelerates fatigue.

### 7.5 Evolution: Towed Array Deployment

**What happens:** The towed array winch pays out the cable and hydrophone array astern. The array streams behind the submarine at the end of a long cable, providing significantly greater detection range but introducing a bearing ambiguity.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **Sonar Supervisor** orders towed array deployment. Reports progress: halfway at 15 seconds, fully deployed at 30 seconds.
- **OOW** ensures speed is at or below 12 kt (maximum deployment speed).
- **Helmsman** maintains steady course during deployment.

**Player command:** Use the towed array panel button to deploy or retract. Deployment takes 30 seconds.

**Operational parameters:**

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Deployment time | 30 seconds |
| Maximum deployment speed | 12 kt |
| Detection range (operational) | ~45 km / 24 nm (4,500 wu) |
| Detection range (damaged) | ~30 km / 16 nm |
| Dead cone | +/-28 degrees from stern |

**Bearing ambiguity:** The towed array cannot distinguish port from starboard. Every detection produces two bearing candidates — one real, one a mirror image reflected across the submarine's heading axis. Both appear on the tactical display as dashed bearing lines.

The hull array resolves this ambiguity. When the hull array independently detects the same contact, the system compares bearings and determines which towed candidate is correct. Resolution requires 1-3 consistent hull-array bearings.

**Standing order:** Do not fire on an unresolved towed-array contact. There is a 50% probability of sending the weapon in the wrong direction.

**Restrictions and hazards:**
- Above 18 kt: cable stressed, array degrades.
- Above 22 kt: cable parts, array destroyed.
- Emergency turn or crash dive: array damaged or destroyed if deployed.
- Type 209: does not carry a towed array.

**Recovery:** The array may be retracted at any time. Retraction takes 20 seconds. A retracted array is protected from speed damage and emergency manoeuvre damage.

### 7.6 Evolution: Active Sonar Ping

**What happens:** The sonar operator transmits a high-power acoustic pulse from the hull-mounted transducer. The pulse propagates outward, reflects off any solid object, and returns as an echo. The system computes range and bearing from the return time and direction.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders "PING ON SONAR." This is a CO-level decision.
- **Sonar Operator** transmits the pulse. Reports range and bearing of all returns.
- **Sonar Supervisor** updates the tactical plot.
- **WEPS** notes the TMA boost — active ping drives solution quality to approximately 0.90.

**Player command:** Press **Space**.

**Parameters:**
- Detection range: approximately 1,800 wu (18 km / 10 nm).
- Direct range and bearing data — the only shipboard system capable of direct range measurement at distance.
- Cooldown: 9 seconds between transmissions.
- Dazzle effect: disrupts enemy torpedo seekers within 1,800 wu for 1.5 seconds.

**Cost — this is critical:**
- An active ping is the loudest emission the submarine can produce.
- A single ping alerts every hostile unit within approximately 5,000 wu (50 km / 27 nm).
- All alerted units receive a precise bearing to the submarine and a large suspicion increase (+0.75).

**When to ping:**
- Confirming range for a weapons solution when passive TMA is insufficient and the tactical situation demands immediate engagement.
- Dazzling incoming torpedo seekers as a last-resort defensive measure when countermeasures have been exhausted.
- Locating contacts in a high-clutter environment where passive detection has failed.

**Standing order:** Routine use of active sonar is operationally inappropriate and likely to be fatal.

### 7.7 Evolution: Periscope Observation

**What happens:** The attack periscope is raised to observation depth. The OOW conducts a visual sweep of the surface environment. Detection sensitivity is boosted by 1.55x against all contacts within visual range. However, the raised periscope creates a visual and radar signature that exposes the submarine's position.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **OOW** orders "UP SCOPE" and conducts the visual sweep.
- **Sonar Supervisor** notes the detection boost. Reports any new contacts.
- **Navigator** plots any visual contacts.
- **OOW** orders "DOWN SCOPE" after the observation period. Maximum 4.5 seconds exposure recommended.

**Player command:** Press **O** to raise/lower the periscope.

**Parameters:**
- Safe operating depth: 22m or shallower.
- Crush depth: 40m — the mast is destroyed if raised below this depth.
- Exposure duration: 4.5 seconds per observation.
- Cooldown: 10 seconds between observations.
- Detection boost: 1.55x against contacts within 3,600 wu (36 km / 19 nm).
- Exposure: reveals the submarine's position to enemies within 3,600 wu.
- Noise transient: +0.10.

**Tactical note:** The periscope should be used in brief, deliberate observations — raise, observe, lower. Extended exposure increases counter-detection risk. UK vessels carry an additional search periscope with a 5-second raise/lower time.

### 7.8 Electronic Support Measures (ESM)

ESM passively detects electromagnetic emissions (radar, radio) from other platforms.

- Range: approximately 12,000 wu (120 km / 65 nm).
- Operating depth: 22m or shallower (mast must be raised).
- Emission: none — ESM is entirely passive and does not reveal the submarine's position.

ESM is the safest mast to raise. It provides long-range awareness of surface threats without any emission risk.

### 7.9 Radar

Active surface-search radar.

- Range: approximately 7,000 wu (70 km / 38 nm).
- Operating depth: 22m or shallower.
- Emission: active — the radar signal is detectable by enemy ESM systems.

Radar provides excellent surface awareness but reveals the submarine's presence. Use with caution.

## Chapter 8 — Acoustic Environment

### 8.1 Thermal Layer

A temperature gradient exists between 180m and 280m depth. This thermocline significantly affects acoustic propagation. Sound crossing the thermal layer boundary loses approximately 40% of its signal strength. This applies equally to the submarine's own noise reaching enemies, enemy noise reaching the submarine, and torpedo seeker ranges when crossing the layer.

**Defensive use:** Operating below the layer (deeper than 280m) while enemies patrol above it provides a significant acoustic advantage. Enemies must close to approximately 60% of their normal detection range to find the submarine.

**Offensive use:** Fire control solutions can be built from below the layer using passive sonar. Torpedoes can be wire-guided across the layer boundary — the wire provides guidance through the acoustic shadow zone until the seeker can acquire the target.

**Layer crossing procedure:** When transiting through the thermocline, the submarine is briefly detectable from both sides. Cross quickly and at minimum noise:
1. Reduce speed to 3-5 kt.
2. Engage silent running.
3. Order the target depth.
4. Cross the 180-280m band as rapidly as plane authority allows.
5. Resume patrol depth on the other side.

### 8.2 Convergence Zone

At distances of approximately 4,800-5,500 wu (48-55 km / 26-30 nm), acoustic energy refracts through the deep water column and converges, creating a detection annulus at extreme range.

**Conditions:** CZ detection requires both platforms to be below the thermal layer. If either platform is above the layer, CZ propagation does not occur.

**Characteristics:**
- Signal boost: approximately 3.2x compared to direct-path at the same range.
- Useful for early warning of approaching threat groups.
- Bearing accuracy is lower than direct-path detection.
- TMA takes significantly longer to build at CZ distances due to minimal bearing rate.

### 8.3 Acoustic Signature Composition

The submarine's acoustic signature is the sum of all noise sources:

| Source | Contribution | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Machinery baseline | Vessel-specific (0.018 to 0.040) | Always present. Determined by hull design and propulsion type. |
| Flow noise | Speed / 32 | The dominant noise source at transit speeds. |
| Propeller tonals | +0.07 per turn | Turning generates additional blade-rate harmonics. |
| Flank speed boost | +0.28 to +0.55 (varies by class) | Running at flank produces significantly more noise than full speed. |
| Cavitation | +0.22 spike | Occurs at speed thresholds that vary by depth and vessel. |
| Transients | Decays at 0.35/s | Produced by manoeuvres, weapon launches, emergency actions. |
| Auxiliary machinery | Varies | HP compressor (+0.55), snorkel (0.35), flooding pumps. |

**Signature reduction measures:**
- Silent running: 0.55x multiplier (45% reduction), speed capped at 8 kt.
- Reduced speed: most effective single measure. Each knot reduction lowers flow noise.
- Depth: operating deeper reduces cavitation risk.
- Smooth manoeuvring: avoid emergency manoeuvres unless necessary. Avoid sudden speed changes.

### 8.4 Cavitation

Cavitation occurs when propeller blade tips create localised low-pressure zones that cause dissolved gas to form bubbles, which collapse violently. The onset speed depends on depth — deeper water has higher ambient pressure, delaying onset.

| Vessel | Cavitation Onset (at reference depth 380m) |
|---|---|
| U-36 (Type 209) | 11 kt |
| HMS Swiftsure | 17 kt |
| USS Dallas (688i) | 18 kt |
| HMS Trafalgar | 20 kt |
| USS Connecticut (Seawolf) | 22 kt |

Cavitation adds a noise spike of +0.22. At shallow depth, even moderate speeds may produce cavitation. Deep operations significantly raise the cavitation threshold.

## Chapter 9 — Target Motion Analysis

### 9.1 Principles

TMA is the process of determining a contact's position, course, and speed from a series of passive bearing observations taken over time. It is the foundation of the fire control solution.

The sonar team triangulates the contact's position by comparing bearings taken from different positions as the submarine moves. Solution quality depends on three factors:

1. **Number of observations** — at least 2 required, 6 or more contribute full quality.
2. **Baseline** — the distance the submarine has moved between its first and most recent observation. Approximately 350 wu (3.5 km / 2 nm) of travel provides full baseline contribution.
3. **Bearing crossing angle** — the angular divergence between observations. This is the critical factor and can only be generated by changing course.

### 9.2 The Manoeuvre Requirement

If the submarine maintains a constant heading, all bearing observations are nearly parallel. The TMA solver cannot triangulate from parallel lines — quality caps at approximately 0.28, well below the firing threshold.

A course change of at least 15-20 degrees is required to generate useful bearing crossing. A change of 30+ degrees produces rapid quality improvement. Two changes in opposite directions (a zig-zag) produce optimal geometry.

**Standard TMA procedure:**
1. Detect contact on bearing. Note the bearing.
2. Continue on current heading for 30-60 seconds at 5-7 kt. This builds baseline.
3. Turn 30-40 degrees. Continue for another 30-60 seconds.
4. Check TMA quality. If DEGRADED, you can fire. If not, turn again.
5. For SOLID, continue manoeuvring. A zig-zag (turn left 30 degrees, drive, turn right 30 degrees) is ideal.

Total time from first detection to SOLID solution: typically 90-180 seconds depending on range and geometry.

### 9.3 Solution Quality

| Quality | Classification | Capability |
|---|---|---|
| Below 0.15 | No solution | Bearing line only. Observation continues. |
| 0.15 - 0.35 | Position estimate | Approximate position shown on display. Not sufficient for weapons. |
| 0.35 - 0.70 | DEGRADED | Range estimate available. Weapons may be fired with wire guidance only — no lead-angle computation. Suitable for close-range engagements. |
| Above 0.70 | SOLID | Full solution. Weapons fired with lead-angle intercept — wire steers the torpedo ahead of the target's projected position. Required for long-range engagements. |

**Solution decay:** If observations cease (contact goes quiet or detection is lost), TMA quality degrades over time. A SOLID solution degrades to DEGRADED in approximately 25-30 seconds without fresh bearings. Bearings older than 150 seconds are discarded entirely.

### 9.4 Contact Classification

As TMA quality builds, the sonar team progressively classifies the contact:

**Stage 1 — Broadband Hull Type (Quality >= 0.20):**
Initial classification from broadband noise characteristics:
- SUBMERGED — submarine contact
- SURFACE — surface vessel
- MERCHANT — commercial vessel (non-combatant)

**Stage 2 — Tonal Analysis (Quality >= 0.35, plus 15-25 seconds accumulation):**
Narrowband frequency analysis identifies the general platform type:
- SSN, SSK, SSBN, SSGN — submarine categories
- FRIGATE, DESTROYER, CORVETTE, CRUISER — surface combatant categories

**Stage 3 — Machinery Signature (Quality >= 0.50, plus 20-40 seconds accumulation):**
Detailed machinery analysis identifies the specific class:
- SSN AKULA, SSK KILO, FRIGATE KRIVAK, etc.

Classification requires sustained quality above the threshold. If TMA quality drops below the required level, the accumulation timer resets.

### 9.5 Evolution: Designating a Contact

**What happens:** The WEPS designates the sonar contact for the Torpedo Data Computer. The TDC begins computing the fire control solution from the contact's TMA data.

**Watchperson actions:**
- **WEPS** announces target designation. The TDC panel updates with the designation and displays current solution quality, bearing, estimated range, and intercept data.
- **Sonar Supervisor** confirms the designation matches the intended contact.
- **OOW** acknowledges and monitors the TDC readout.

**Player command:** Left-click on the contact's bearing line or position blob on the tactical display. The clicked contact becomes the TDC target. The TDC panel on the HUD displays the current solution quality and firing data.

**Detection zones for click designation:**
- Contact blob: +/-36 screen pixels.
- Bearing line: +/-10 screen pixels (tighter for accuracy).
- Torpedo: +/-36 screen pixels (for wire guidance designation).

### 10.3 Wire Guidance

All torpedoes are wire-guided from launch. A thin guidance wire connects the torpedo to the submarine's fire control system, allowing the TDC to steer the weapon toward the target.

**Auto-TDC mode (default):**
- At DEGRADED quality: wire steers toward the contact's latest bearing — no lead-angle prediction. Effective at close range.
- At SOLID quality: wire computes a lead angle using bearing rate and estimated time-of-flight. The torpedo is steered ahead of the target's projected position. Dramatically higher hit rate at range.

**Wire stress table:**

| Speed | Wire Status |
|---|---|
| 0-15 kt | No stress. Normal operations. |
| 15-20 kt | Wire under stress. Parts in approximately 25 seconds. |
| 20-22 kt | Severe stress. Parts in approximately 3 seconds. |
| Above 22 kt | Wire parts immediately. |
| Emergency turn | All wires severed immediately. |
| Crash dive | All wires severed immediately. |
| Wire range exceeded (~3,000 wu) | Wire parts automatically. |

**Wire-to-seeker handoff:** When the torpedo approaches the target and its onboard seeker detects the target within its acquisition cone, the seeker takes over guidance. The wire is no longer needed — the torpedo homes autonomously.

**Multiple wires:** Multiple torpedoes can be in the water simultaneously, each on its own wire. The fire control system steers each independently toward its designated target. The speed constraint applies to all live wires collectively.

### 10.4 Manual Wire Control

Individual torpedo wires can be manually controlled from the wire panel:

- **Auto TDC mode** (default): fire control steers the weapon automatically. Toggle off for manual control.
- **Nudge:** Manual bearing adjustment (+/- degrees) when auto TDC is off.
- **Wire cut:** Deliberately sever the wire, releasing the torpedo to its seeker or search pattern.
- **Tube selection:** Click a tube to select it for the next shot.

**When wires break (from any cause):**
- The torpedo continues on its last steered heading.
- If the seeker has a lock, it homes normally.
- If no lock, it enters a search snake pattern along its last heading.

### 10.5 Seeker Mechanics

**Passive seeker:** Broad field of view (2.1-2.5 radians depending on weapon type). Homes on the target's acoustic signature.

**Active seeker:** Narrower field of view (0.75-0.95 radians). Transmits its own acoustic pulse and homes on the return.

**Acquisition range:** 340-600 wu depending on weapon type.

**Reacquisition after loss:** If the torpedo loses lock, it enters a reacquisition delay (4.0-8.5 seconds depending on weapon type), then executes a hook manoeuvre: breaks 45 degrees away from the last known position, hooks 125 degrees back to cross the original target track, and resumes a search snake pattern.

**Depth capability:** All torpedoes have a depth rate of 2 m/s and a vertical search window of 120m. Torpedo seekers can engage targets at different depths within this window.

## Chapter 12 — Countermeasures

### 12.1 Noisemaker Decoy

The primary defensive countermeasure against incoming torpedoes. The decoy produces a loud acoustic signature that competes with the submarine for the torpedo seeker's attention.

### 12.2 Evolution: Countermeasure Defence Against Incoming Torpedo

**What happens:** The sonar team reports an incoming torpedo. The OOW orders countermeasure deployment, speed reduction, and evasion. The decoy creates a noise source that may seduce the torpedo away from the submarine.

**Watchperson actions:**
1. **Sonar Supervisor** reports "TORPEDO IN THE WATER, BEARING [XXX]."
2. **OOW** orders "DEPLOY COUNTERMEASURE. RIG FOR SILENT RUNNING. ALL AHEAD CREEP."
3. **Countermeasure Operator** deploys noisemaker.
4. **Helmsman** reduces speed to 3 kt.
5. **OOW** orders course change to open distance from the decoy.
6. **Diving Officer** orders depth change to cross the thermal layer if feasible.
7. **All hands** await the outcome. Monitor the torpedo track on the tactical display.

**Player commands — in order:**
1. Press **X** to deploy noisemaker.
2. Press **Z** to engage silent running (if not already active).
3. Press **A** repeatedly to reduce speed to 3 kt.
4. Click a new waypoint to change heading — put distance between the submarine and the decoy.
5. Press **S** to increase depth (cross below the thermal layer if above it, or gain depth separation).

**Seduction probability — the critical equation:**

The quieter the submarine at the moment of countermeasure deployment, the more likely the torpedo will be seduced away. The decoy must acoustically outcompete the submarine — if the submarine is louder than the decoy, the torpedo may ignore it.

| Submarine Noise Level | Seduction Probability |
|---|---|
| Very quiet (creep + silent running, noise ~0.07) | ~90% |
| Quiet (slow patrol, noise ~0.15) | ~75% |
| Moderate (normal patrol, noise ~0.25) | ~64% |
| Loud (moderate speed, noise ~0.40) | ~43% |
| Minimum floor (regardless of noise) | 15% |

**Post-seduction behaviour:** After the torpedo has been seduced and the decoy expires:
- The torpedo enters a reacquisition delay (4.0-8.5 seconds depending on torpedo type).
- It executes a hook manoeuvre: breaks 45 degrees away from the decoy's last position, hooks 125 degrees back to cross the original target track.
- It resumes a search snake pattern.

This reacquisition delay is the survival window. During this time the submarine must open range, change depth, or cross the thermal layer.

### 12.3 Active Sonar as Defensive Countermeasure

An active ping dazzles enemy torpedo seekers within 1,800 wu for 1.5 seconds. This can break a lock on an incoming torpedo, buying time for the seeker's reacquisition delay.

**When to use:** Only when a torpedo is in its terminal approach phase and noisemaker countermeasures have failed or are exhausted. The acoustic cost is extreme — every enemy within 5,000 wu receives a precise bearing and +0.75 suspicion.

**Player command:** Press **Space** when the incoming torpedo is within 1,800 wu.

## Chapter 13 — Hull Structure and Compartmentation

### 13.1 Watertight Sections

The submarine hull is divided into six watertight sections (WTS), separated by watertight doors. Each section contains multiple compartments across internal decks.

| WTS | Designation | Complement | Primary Systems |

Total complement: approximately 88 personnel.

Some compartments within each section are unmanned during normal operations. Casualties in unmanned compartments (particularly fire) may go undetected for 30-60 seconds until sensor alarms trigger or watchkeepers notice secondary indications.

### 13.2 Watertight Doors

Five WTDs separate adjacent watertight sections:

| Door | Between |
|---|---|
| WTD 1 | WTS 1 (Fore Ends) — WTS 2 (Control Room) |
| WTD 2 | WTS 2 (Control Room) — WTS 3 (Mess Decks) |
| WTD 3 | WTS 3 (Mess Decks) — WTS 4 (Reactor Compartment) |
| WTD 4 | WTS 4 (Reactor Compartment) — WTS 5 (Engine Room) |
| WTD 5 | WTS 5 (Engine Room) — WTS 6 (Aft Ends) |

**Door states:**
- **Open:** Normal condition. Crew transits freely. Flooding and fire spread unimpeded between sections.
- **Closed:** Flooding and fire spread between sections is slowed. Crew transit is blocked except through the reactor tunnel bypass.

**Hydraulic dependency:** WTD operation requires the main hydraulic plant (located in WTS 2, Control Room). If the hydraulic plant is destroyed, doors cannot be operated.

**Reactor tunnel bypass:** WTDs adjacent to the reactor compartment (WTD 3 and WTD 4) have a bypass tunnel through the reactor D1 top deck passage. Crew transit through these doors is never blocked, even when closed. This ensures DC teams can always reach the aft sections of the boat.

### 13.3 Shipboard Systems

Every major system is tracked individually and can be in one of four states:

| State | Operational Effect | Approximate Repair Time |
|---|---|---|
| Nominal | Full capability | — |
| Degraded | Reduced capability — system-specific effects | ~20 seconds |
| Offline | System non-functional | ~45 seconds |
| Destroyed | Non-functional — longest repair time | ~120 seconds |

**Key systems and their degraded effects:**

| System | Location | Effect When Degraded/Offline |
|---|---|---|
| Sonar Array | WTS 1 | Reduced passive detection range |
| Torpedo Tubes | WTS 1 | Reduced tube availability / offline = no firing |
| TMA Computer | WTS 1 | Degraded TMA quality / no TMA |
| Fire Control | WTS 2 | Degraded wire guidance accuracy / wire cut / no firing |
| Helm | WTS 2 | Reduced steering authority |
| Ballast Control | WTS 2 | Degraded depth control / emergency blow automatic mechanism fails |
| Main Hydraulic Plant | WTS 2 | WTD operation lost / plane control degraded |
| Reactor | WTS 4 | Power output reduced / SCRAM risk |

| Main Turbines | WTS 5 | Speed restrictions |
| Steering | WTS 6 | Reduced turn rate |
| Aft Planes | WTS 6 | Degraded depth control |

**Control node dependencies:** Some systems depend on a control node. If the control node is degraded or worse, the dependent system's effective state is the worst of its own and the control node's.
- Torpedo tubes depend on fire control.
- Forward planes depend on helm.
- TDC depends on fire control.

### 14.2 Evolution: Emergency Stations (Called Automatically on Hit)

**What happens:** A weapon impact triggers emergency stations automatically. The casualty state changes from NORMAL to EMERGENCY. DC teams muster and auto-dispatch to casualties. Medical teams activate. Watertight doors auto-close in affected sections.

**Watchperson actions:**
1. **OOW** announces "EMERGENCY STATIONS, EMERGENCY STATIONS" to all compartments.

5. **Both watches** called to stations. All hands to action stations.

2. **Fire** — second priority. Teams fight fires with extinguishers and the fixed drench system.
3. **System repair** — lowest priority. Teams repair damaged systems.

**Transit times:** Teams must travel to the casualty location. Transit time ranges from 10 seconds (adjacent section) to 56 seconds (opposite end of the boat). Closed WTDs block crew transit between sections except through the reactor tunnel bypass.

- DC teams operate autonomously. The CO does not issue direct orders to DC teams.

## Chapter 15 — Flooding Casualties

### 15.1 Evolution: Flooding From Torpedo Hit

**What happens:** A torpedo impact breaches the hull. Seawater enters the damaged section at a rate proportional to breach severity and current depth (deeper water = higher ambient pressure = faster flooding). Flooding progresses from 0 to 1.0 scale per compartment. Water spreads through open watertight doors to adjacent sections.

**Watchperson actions:**
1. **Affected section watchstanders** report the breach to Damage Control Central.
2. **Chief of the Watch** monitors ballast tank fill and trim indicators.
3. **DC teams** auto-dispatch to the flooding casualty. Shore up the breach and operate bilge pumps.
4. **Diving Officer** reports depth control status. Compensates with planes and ballast.
5. **OOW** assesses the overall flooding rate and decides whether emergency blow is required.

**Effects by severity:**

| Sections Flooded | Sink Rate | Assessment |
|---|---|---|
| 1 section | Controllable | Depth planes can compensate at moderate speed. |
| 2 sections | ~2.0 m/s | Planes may not hold without emergency blow. Boat is heavy. |
| 3 sections | ~3.0 m/s | The boat is almost certainly lost unless shallow and immediate blow is executed. |

**Additional flooding effects:**
- Trim imbalance: forward or aft flooding creates a pitch moment.
- System damage: equipment in flooded compartments degrades through offline to destroyed.
- Crew casualties: personnel in flooded sections are killed or injured.
- Acoustic penalty: bilge pumps add to the submarine's noise signature.

**Player response:**
1. Reduce speed to 7-10 kt. This provides plane authority without broadcasting position.
2. Check depth. If deep, consider coming shallower to reduce flooding rate. But maintain thermal layer cover if possible.
3. Press **H** to view the damage screen. Monitor flooding progress and DC team status.
4. Do not execute emergency manoeuvres after a hit unless absolutely necessary — the SCRAM risk from combined manoeuvres at speed compounds the flooding danger.
5. If flooding is overwhelming plane authority or depth is approaching the collapse envelope, execute emergency blow.

### 15.2 Evolution: Emergency Blow

**What happens:** The OOW orders emergency blow. The HPA system vents high-pressure air directly into the main ballast tanks, forcing water out and creating positive buoyancy. The reserve bank is committed if the group bank alone is insufficient.

**Watchperson actions:**
1. **OOW** orders "EMERGENCY BLOW."
2. **Chief of the Watch** activates the emergency blow mechanism. The system checks HPA pressure against ambient.
3. If HPA pressure is sufficient (group bank + reserve bank > ambient pressure x 1.2), air flows into the tanks.
4. If ballast control system is damaged, the automatic mechanism fails — DC teams operate the valves manually, adding approximately 12 seconds of delay.
5. If total available pressure is below ambient, the blow fails — insufficient air to displace the water.
6. **Diving Officer** monitors tank fill. Tanks are considered clear when average fill drops below 2%.
7. The submarine becomes positively buoyant and rises.
8. **OOW** monitors ascent. At or near the surface, HPA recharges automatically.

**Player command:** Use the emergency blow button on the damage control panel.

**HPA pressure vs depth — can the blow succeed?**

| Depth | Ambient Pressure | HPA Required (1.2x ratio) |
|---|---|---|
| 100m | 10 bar | 12 bar |
| 200m | 20 bar | 24 bar |
| 300m | 30 bar | 36 bar |
| 400m | 40 bar | 48 bar |

Total available: up to 207 bar (group) + 207 bar (reserve) = 414 bar at full charge. However, sustained combat (torpedo launches at 2 bar each, ballast adjustments, previous blow attempts) may have depleted the banks significantly.

**Cancellation:** Emergency blow is cancelled by issuing any new depth order. Do not press **W** or **S** after ordering a blow unless intentionally cancelling. The blow also cancels when the submarine reaches 20m or shallower.

**Post-blow:**
- The submarine surfaces or reaches shallow depth.
- HPA recharges automatically at the surface.
- The submarine is exposed to radar, visual detection, and surface weapons.
- The blow itself generates a noise transient of approximately +0.55.

## Chapter 16 — Fire Casualties

### 16.1 Evolution: Fire From Torpedo Hit

**What happens:** The weapon impact ignites combustible material in compartments near the hit location. Fire intensity increases over time if unchecked. Fire spreads to adjacent compartments within the same watertight section. Equipment in burning compartments degrades. Personnel suffer casualties from heat and toxic gases.

**Watchperson actions:**
1. **Manned compartments:** Fire is reported immediately by the crew on station.
2. **Unmanned compartments:** Fire burns undetected for 30-60 seconds until sensor alarms trigger or smoke reaches a manned space.
3. **DC teams** auto-dispatch to the fire. Fight with portable extinguishers.
4. **DC Coordinator** assesses fire severity. If the fire cannot be contained with portable equipment:
5. **Chief of the Watch** activates the fixed drench system (N2 drench) for the affected compartment. 20-second drench countdown.
6. **OOW** orders evacuation of affected compartments if fire intensity threatens personnel.

**Drench system effects:**
- The drenched compartment becomes uninhabitable — nitrogen displaces oxygen.
- The compartment must be vented before crew can return.
- The drench is highly effective at suppressing fire but makes the compartment temporarily unusable for all purposes.

**Player response:**
1. Press **H** to view the damage screen. Identify the fire location and severity.
2. Press **J** or **Tab** to view the DC LOG for fire suppression reports.
3. DC teams handle fire suppression automatically. No direct player action is required for firefighting.
4. Monitor fire spread. If fire reaches a section with critical systems (reactor, propulsion), the cascading effects may be severe.

## Chapter 17 — Crew Casualties

### 17.1 Watch System

The crew is divided into Watch A and Watch B. One watch is on duty while the other rests.

- **Fatigue:** The on-watch crew fatigues over time, degrading sonar detection probability by up to 40%.

### 17.2 Personnel Casualties

Personnel are killed or injured by flooding, fire, and weapon explosions.

- **Killed:** Permanently lost. No replacement available.

**Effects of crew loss in a section:**
- Systems in unmanned compartments go unmonitored.
- Fire detection in those compartments is delayed.
- Damage reports from that section cease.
- The section's contribution to ship operations is reduced.
- Maximum available DC team strength is reduced.

## Chapter 18 — High-Pressure Air System

### 18.1 System Overview

HPA is the compressed air system providing energy for three critical functions:
1. **Ballast tank control** — adjusting MBT fill to control buoyancy and depth.
2. **Torpedo launch** — impulse air to eject weapons from torpedo tubes (2 bar per shot).
3. **Emergency blow** — forcing water out of ballast tanks to arrest an uncontrolled descent.

### 18.2 Pressure Banks

| Bank | Capacity | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Group Bank | 207 bar | Normal operations: ballast control, torpedo launch |
| Reserve Bank | 207 bar | Emergency use only. Isolated until committed during emergency blow. |

**Pressure vs depth:** Ambient water pressure increases at approximately 1 bar per 10 metres (0.1 bar per metre). HPA pressure must exceed ambient by a factor of 1.2 for full ballast control authority.

If HPA pressure drops below the required threshold, the submarine cannot drain its ballast tanks — it can flood them (increase negative buoyancy) but cannot empty them (increase positive buoyancy). This is a critical constraint during deep operations and after sustained combat.

### 18.3 Consumption

- Ballast adjustments: consumed during normal depth changes.
- Torpedo launch: 2 bar per shot.
- Emergency blow: consumed at a rate proportional to the pressure differential — rapid consumption at depth.

### 18.4 Recharge

HPA can only be recharged at or near the surface (20m or shallower):

| Method | Rate | Noise |
|---|---|---|
| LP compressor | 0.4 bar/s | Silent. Operates continuously at the surface. |
| HP compressor | 2.5 bar/s | +0.55 noise per second. Operator-initiated. |

The reserve bank recharges at a reduced rate compared to the group bank.

## Chapter 19 — Combined Damage Scenarios

### 19.1 Surviving the First Hit

The first hit is survivable in all vessels. CO priorities, in order:

1. **Maintain adequate speed for plane authority.** 7-10 kt gives plane authority without excessive noise.
2. **Monitor depth.** Arrest any descent before reaching the collapse envelope.
3. **Assess damage.** Press **H** to view the damage screen. Identify flooding, fire, and system damage.
4. **Do not panic-manoeuvre.** Emergency turns and crash dives after a hit risk SCRAM (especially if an emergency manoeuvre was executed before the hit). A SCRAM at depth with flooding is usually fatal.
5. **Maintain noise discipline.** Flooding pumps are loud, but sinking is worse.

### 19.2 Surviving the Second Hit

Two hits are survivable but dangerous.

- Two sections flooding creates approximately 2.0 m/s sink rate. Planes can fight this with speed, but the boat is heavy.
- Emergency blow may be required. Check HPA pressure on the damage panel.
- Two hits have consumed torpedo impulse air and ballast control pressure. At depth, available HPA may be insufficient to overcome ambient pressure.
- Crush depth has been reduced by weapon damage — the crew receives no precise indication of the new limit.

### 19.3 Surviving the Third Hit

Three sections flooding creates approximately 3.0 m/s sink rate. The boat is almost certainly lost unless:
- The submarine is already shallow (< 150m).
- Emergency blow can be executed with sufficient HPA.
- The flooding is in non-adjacent sections (reducing trim impact).

At this point the CO's priority is escape, not combat.

### 19.4 SCRAM at Depth With Active Flooding

This is the most dangerous combined casualty. The reactor is offline. Propulsion is limited to 3 kt on the EPM. Plane authority at 3 kt is marginal. Depth control relies almost entirely on buoyancy — which is being lost to flooding.

**CO response:**
1. Emergency blow immediately if not already ordered.
2. Accept the noise penalty — survival takes precedence over concealment.
3. If the blow fails (insufficient HPA), the situation may be unrecoverable.
4. Wait 75 seconds for reactor restart. If the reactor recovers before depth reaches the collapse envelope, increase speed for plane authority and arrest the descent.

## Chapter 22 — Enemy Doctrine

### 22.1 Detection and Prosecution

Soviet submarines use passive sonar with shorter effective range and wider baffle zones than NATO equivalents. Soviet machinery is significantly louder — noise baseline 0.62 to 0.90 compared to 0.018-0.040 for NATO boats.

Enemy contacts are prosecuted through a suspicion-based system:

| Suspicion Level | Enemy Behaviour |
|---|---|
| Below 0.22 | No investigation. Contact lost or dismissed. |
| 0.22 - 0.78 | Investigation. Enemy manoeuvres toward the datum to establish contact. |
| Above 0.78 | Engagement. Enemy positions for a weapons solution and fires. |

**Suspicion decay:**
- Base decay: 0.003 per second.
- Bonus decay (when submarine noise < 0.14): additional 0.006 per second.
- Total decay when quiet: 0.009 per second.

At quiet noise levels, enemy suspicion decays approximately twice as fast. This makes going quiet the most reliable method of breaking contact.

### 22.2 Wolfpack Coordination

Soviet submarines operating within approximately 3,500 wu of each other share tactical data. The coordination operates in waves:

**Wave 1:** Two hunters. Passive prosecution only.
**Wave 2:** One pinger + two hunters. The pinger uses active sonar to fix the target's position and broadcasts the datum to the hunters.
**Wave 3:** One pinger + two hunters + two interceptors. Interceptors move to cut off escape routes using projected track data.

**Wave spawn delay:** 30 seconds between waves.

**Counter-wolfpack doctrine — CO's recommended response:**
1. Kill the pinger first. Without active sonar datum broadcasts, the hunters must rely on their own degraded passive sonar.
2. Engage from below the thermal layer. Torpedoes cross the layer with wire guidance. Enemy sonar has 40% less range looking down.
3. Use range. Fire at maximum wire range (~3,000 wu). Enemy return fire at this range has wide TMA blur.
4. Reposition after every shot. The launch transient gives them a bearing. Change heading by 60+ degrees and open range.
5. Prioritise: pinger > nearest hunter > interceptor.

### 22.3 ASW Surface Ship Doctrine

Surface warships follow structured ASW prosecution:

- **Active sonar prosecution:** Initiated when suspicion exceeds a moderate threshold (0.55).
- **Hull sonar:** Effective range approximately 1,600 wu. Limited to above the thermal layer.
- **Variable Depth Sonar (VDS):** Range approximately 2,400 wu. The VDS is lowered beneath the thermocline — can detect below the thermal layer.
- **ASROC missile torpedo:** Rocket-delivered torpedo that splashes near the estimated contact position and begins circular search. Range 300-2,800 wu. Deploys at 45m depth.

### 22.4 Baffle Clearing

Certain enemy submarine types (hunter, interceptor, and AKULA-class) periodically execute baffle-clearing manoeuvres:
- Turn 35 degrees off base heading.
- Hold the cleared heading for 20-30 seconds.
- Return to base heading.
- Interval: every 90-150 seconds.

Do not assume the enemy's baffle zone provides permanent concealment.

### 22.5 Enemy Adaptation

Enemies learn from missed engagements. Each torpedo that fails to hit tightens their subsequent fire control accuracy by approximately 15% (to a floor of 50% of original error).

This adaptation resets if:
- 60 seconds pass without another engagement.
- The target changes heading by more than 30 degrees.

Repeated engagements against the same enemy group without repositioning become progressively more dangerous.

## Chapter 23 — Tactical Doctrine

### 23.1 Standard Patrol Posture

- **Depth:** 280-320m (below the thermal layer).
- **Speed:** 3-5 kt.
- **Silent running:** Engaged when contacts are present.
- **Towed array:** Deployed (if equipped).
- **Watch:** Rotate to manage fatigue.

This posture provides maximum passive sonar range, minimum acoustic signature, and thermal layer protection against surface and shallow-water threats.

### 23.2 Evolution: Breaking Contact

When the submarine has been detected and enemies are prosecuting:

1. Go deep (below layer) and slow (3-5 kt, silent running).
2. Turn perpendicular to the enemy's bearing — put them on the beam, not the stern.
3. Wait. Suspicion decays at 0.003/s base, plus 0.006/s bonus when noise is below 0.14.
4. At suspicion below 0.22, they stop investigating and return to patrol.

**Player commands:** Press **S** to go deep. Press **A** to reduce speed. Press **Z** for silent running. Click a new waypoint to change heading.

### 23.3 Evolution: Sprint and Clear

1. Sprint at flank speed on a heading away from the threat.
2. After 30-60 seconds, reduce to 3 kt and engage silent running.
3. Change heading by 90+ degrees.
4. Resume quiet patrol.

This works because range is opened during the sprint (enemies lose contact), then the submarine goes quiet at a position the enemy does not expect. The heading change prevents track projection.

### 23.4 Evolution: Thermal Layer Exploit

1. If above the layer, crash dive through it (press **C**).
2. Once below 280m, reduce to 3 kt, engage silent running.
3. The layer now blocks 40% of enemy detection — they may lose contact entirely.

This is the most reliable evasion technique. It works even against persistent enemies.

### 23.5 Type 209 — Special Tactical Considerations

The Type 209 operates under unique constraints that require modified doctrine:

- **Start at 190m** (safe diving depth). Above the thermal layer but deep enough to reduce detection.
- **Patrol at 3 kt.** Noise floor of 0.018 is below enemy detection thresholds at moderate range.
- **Use the layer from above.** The boat cannot go below it (250m DL vs 280m layer floor), but enemies diving below it have their sonar degraded by 40% looking up.
- **Fire at close range.** With only 14 torpedoes, wait for SOLID solutions at < 2,000 wu.
- **Disengage after firing.** Go to 3 kt, silent running, change heading immediately.
- **Manage battery religiously.** Never let charge drop below 20% unless snorkel is assured within minutes.
- **Snorkel only when clear.** Conduct a full passive listen at 3 kt before surfacing to 12m.

### 23.6 Multi-Target Engagement

With wire guidance, multiple torpedoes can be in the water on different targets simultaneously. Each tube maintains its own wire.

**Staggered salvo procedure:**
1. Build SOLID TMA on the first target.
2. Fire torpedo 1 at Target A (press **F**).
3. Immediately designate Target B (click on it).
4. Wait for DEGRADED or better on Target B.
5. Fire torpedo 2 at Target B (press **F**).

Both torpedoes are wire-guided independently. Fire control steers each toward its designated target.

**Speed constraint:** Must stay below 15 kt while wires are live. Plan the firing position to provide: thermal layer between submarine and enemy, range advantage, and an escape route that does not require sprinting through the wire field.

### 23.7 Convergence Zone Exploitation

CZ contacts appear at 4,800-5,500 wu. Use for early warning and long-range engagement planning:

1. Detect via CZ. Sonar reports the contact in the convergence zone.
2. Build TMA. This is slow at CZ range — bearing change is minimal. Aggressive manoeuvring helps.
3. Close to firing range. CZ range is too far for reliable torpedo engagement. Close to 2,000-3,000 wu.
4. Alternatively, use missiles against surface contacts at any range.

CZ detection requires both platforms below the thermal layer.
